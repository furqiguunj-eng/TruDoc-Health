F · The First 90 Days article
LinkedIn long-form · Mid-week · ~900 words
By Dr. Ashish Kumar Jaiswal, COO, TruDoc Healthcare

Bridging the digital-physical divide in regional healthcare

Every region's healthcare transformation eventually arrives at the same crossroads. The technology works. The patients are willing. The capital is available. And yet the integrated experience — the one that treats a patient as a continuous person rather than a series of disconnected episodes — refuses to materialise.

The GCC is at exactly that crossroads today.

We do not have a technology problem. Teleconsultation is mature. Diagnostics are excellent. Pharmacies are well-distributed. Home health exists. Chronic disease programmes exist. The components are world-class.

What we have is an integration problem. And integration is not a feature you build. It is an operational discipline you sustain.

The three failures of scale

In a decade of operating large healthcare businesses across this region, I have watched ambitious organisations collide with the same three failures every time scale is attempted.

First, the patient experience fractures. Each vertical optimises for its own metric. The teleconsultation team measures consultation time. The pharmacy measures fulfillment speed. The labs measure throughput. Individually defensible, collectively incoherent. The patient feels it long before any dashboard shows it.

Second, accountability dilutes. When five service lines touch a single patient journey, the question "who owns this outcome?" becomes uncomfortable. The natural response is to make ownership collective, which is to say, to make it nobody's. Documented accountability — the kind where a named person is responsible for a named outcome on a named timeline — is the antidote.

Third, the strategy-to-operation gap widens. This is the failure mode that ends most healthcare scaling stories. The strategy deck and the operational reality are written in different languages, audited by different people, and reconciled, if at all, once a year. By the time the gap is visible, it is structural.

What I will be focused on in the first 90 days

I am not arriving with a finished plan. Anyone who claims to is either lying or about to do damage. What I will be focused on is this:

Listening to the operation. Spending time inside each of the five verticals — teleconsultation, pharmacy, labs, home health, chronic disease management — to understand where the friction actually lives, not where the deck says it lives.

Mapping the patient journey end-to-end. Not as a marketing artefact. As an operational document with named owners and measured handoffs at every transition point.

Building the operational accountability layer. Defining what "documented accountability" means at TruDoc, in writing, with the kind of clarity that makes good performance visible and poor performance unmistakable.

Surfacing the unspoken constraints. Every organisation has them. They are usually known to everyone and named by no one. Naming them is half the work.

Why TruDoc, why now

I joined because of a conversation. Vish Narain told me, in plain language, that vision was no longer the constraint at TruDoc — execution was. That is the rarest sentence I have heard from a healthcare chairman in this region. It is the sentence that signals an organisation ready for operational seriousness.

The GCC will produce one or two genuinely integrated, virtual-first healthcare operators over the next five years. TruDoc has the patient trust, the capital, the regulatory relationships, and the strategic clarity to be one of them.

What it will take from here is the discipline of execution. That is the work I am here to do.
